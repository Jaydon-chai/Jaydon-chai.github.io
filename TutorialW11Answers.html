Q4.Describe the general anatomy of an airway sensorynerve i.e. termination sites and cell body location.

Airway sensory neurons have their cell bodies located in the vagal sensoryganglia and consist of a peripheral termination in the airways (via thevagusnerve) and a central termination in the medulla, specifically the nucleus ofthe solitary tract.

Q2.Describe the basic breathing mechanics and includewhich muscles are important for breathing? How isquiet breathing different to deep breathing?

Q2.Describe the basic breathing mechanics and includewhich muscles are important for breathing? How isquiet breathing different to deep breathing?

Quiet breathing

  • Diaphragm–inhalation: diaphragm moves down (inferiorly),increasing volume of thoracic cavity. Exhalation: relaxes allowingnatural elasticity of lungs and ribs to contract and reducevolume.
  • External intercostal muscles–move ribs in asuperio-lateraldirection increasing the volume of thoracic cavity. Exhalation:relaxes allowing natural elasticity of lungs and ribs to contract.
  • Increased volume leads to lower pressure (via Boyles Law) andinflow of air. Decreased volume leads to high pressure andoutflow of air.

Q2.Describe the basic breathing mechanics and includewhich muscles are important for breathing? How isquiet breathing different to deep breathing?

  • For deeper breathing: Any muscle attaching to the ribs can change thedimensions of the thoracic cavity
    • Internal intercostal muscles and abdomen–for deeper exhalation.
    • Muscle in the neck–for deeper inhalation

Q3.Consider how hypercapnia (increase in carbondioxide in arterial blood and cerebrospinal fluid) wouldbe detected and what the result in breathing would be.

Q3.Consider how hypercapnia (increase in carbondioxide in arterial blood and cerebrospinal fluid) wouldbe detected and what the result in breathing would be.

Hypercapnia is detected by monitoring levels of CO2 andpH.pH isdirectly related to CO2 as it reacts with water to produce H+ ions.

Centrally, chemosensory for pH are found on the ventrolateral aspect ofthe medulla, inside the blood-brain barrier. pH is detected in thecerebrospinal fluid, due to diffusion of CO2 from blood. These signals aresent to the DRG. This is main and faster pathway.

A second pathway occurs peripherally:chemosensorsfor CO2 and H+ arefound in the aortic arch and the carotid body and signals are sent to theDRG via thevagusnerve (aortic arch) and glossopharyngeal nerve(carotid body).

The DRG acts to increase respiration to expel excess CO2.
